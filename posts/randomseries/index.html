Random analytic functions: Ryll-Nardzewski's theorem

April 2021

In 1896, Émile Borel asked what can happen at the border of the disk of convergence of a Taylor series:

Étant donné une série de Taylor, il est intéressant de savoir si elle peut être prolongée en quelque manière au-delà de son cercle de convergence ou si ce cercle est une coupure.

This is a vast and difficult question, but surprisingly enough, it has an answer for random Taylor series, which are series whose coefficients are random.

At first sight, it might seem difficult to study the behaviour of a function which is itself random, and one might be tempted to say that random objects have random behaviours; but as Borel hinted in his 1896 paper, randomness comes with averaging, and averaging rules out eccentricity. That's the surprising result I would like to show in this note: this result essentially says that most random functions cannot be extended outside of their disk of convergence.

The precise statement is below; it is due to the Polish mathematician Czesław Ryll-Nardzewski in 1953.

Hadamard's formula

Let us remind a basic fact on the radius of convergence: if f(z)=∑anznf(z)=\sum a_n z^nf(z)=∑an​zn is a power series, its radius of convergence is given by Hadamard's formula,

Inside the disk D(0,r)D(0,r)D(0,r), the power series converges, but this says nothing on what happens on the border of the disk or outside. Does f(z)f(z)f(z) approach a limit as zzz approaches the border, for instance if z→1z\to 1z→1? Does it erratically diverge? Are there some points on the boudary, such that fff can be extended analytically around this point?

Think about the series ∑zn\sum z^n∑zn. Its radius of convergence is 1, but since the sum is equal to (1−z)−1(1-z)^{-1}(1−z)−1, we can extend it at any point of the circle of radius 1, except at the point z=1z=1z=1; in fact, we can extend this function at every point of C∖1\mathbb{C}\setminus 1C∖1, even if the series representation only holds in the disk D(0,1)D(0,1)D(0,1).

What happens at the border

We say that a complex number www on the circle C1={∣z∣=1}C_1 = \{|z|=1\}C1​={∣z∣=1} is regular if there is a δ>0\delta > 0δ>0 such that fff can be extended analytically on D(w,δ)D(w, \delta)D(w,δ).   • If f(z)=∑n=0∞(1−z)−1f(z) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty (1-z)^{-1}f(z)=∑n=0∞​(1−z)−1, then the only non-regular point is 111, thus R=C1∖1\mathscr{R} = C_1 \setminus 1R=C1​∖1. Building on this example can construct functions with any finite number of singular points.

Random analytic functions

(1) Either the circle C1C_1C1​ is a coupure of fff.

A Poisson example

I recently stumbled across an example illustrating this: consider the case where XnX_nXn​ are independent Poisson random variables with parameter dnd^ndn for some common ddd.
