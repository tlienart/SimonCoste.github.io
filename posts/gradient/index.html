Gradient descent over convex landscapes

April 2022

A function fff is convex when f(x)−f(y)⩾⟨∇f(y),x−y⟩f(x) - f(y) \geqslant \langle \nabla f(y), x-y\ranglef(x)−f(y)⩾⟨∇f(y),x−y⟩; with this pretty loose definition, it can very well happen that two different points x,yx,yx,y have the same value f(x)=f(y)f(x) = f(y)f(x)=f(y). Finding a minimum of fff with gradient descent can then lead to very different behaviours. For smooth functions, we have more quantitative notions of convexity and they lead to convergence results for the gradient descent algorithm.

Quantitative convexity

The largest eigenvalues of the Hessian of fff determine the Lipschitz smoothness of fff, while the smallest ones determine the strong convexity of fff. More precisely, if λmin⁡(x),λmax⁡(x)\lambda_{\min}(x), \lambda_{\max}(x)λmin​(x),λmax​(x) are the smallest and largest eigenvalues of ∇2f(x)\nabla^2 f(x)∇2f(x) (the Hessian of fff at point xxx, a square matrix), then by elementary arguments we have

λmin⁡(x)∣v∣2⩽⟨v,∇2f(x)v⟩⩽λmax⁡(x)∣v∣2. \lambda_{\min}(x)|v|^2 \leqslant \langle v, \nabla^2 f(x)v\rangle \leqslant \lambda_{\max}(x)|v|^2. λmin​(x)∣v∣2⩽⟨v,∇2f(x)v⟩⩽λmax​(x)∣v∣2.

But then, if we fix two vectors x,yx,yx,y, using Taylor's formulas yields the following result.

Let fff be a C2\mathscr{C}^2C2, convex function. If there are two numbers 0<η⩽M0< \eta \leqslant M0<η⩽M such that for every xxx, we have η⩽λmin⁡(x)\eta \leqslant \lambda_{\min}(x)η⩽λmin​(x) and λmax⁡(x)⩽M\lambda_{\max}(x)\leqslant Mλmax​(x)⩽M, then for every x,yx,yx,y, ⟨∇f(x),y−x⟩+η2∣x−y∣2⩽f(y)−f(x)⩽⟨∇f(x),y−x⟩+M2∣x−y∣2. \langle \nabla f(x), y-x\rangle + \frac{\eta}{2}|x-y|^2 \leqslant f(y) - f(x) \leqslant \langle \nabla f(x), y-x\rangle + \frac{M}{2}|x-y|^2. ⟨∇f(x),y−x⟩+2η​∣x−y∣2⩽f(y)−f(x)⩽⟨∇f(x),y−x⟩+2M​∣x−y∣2.

The lower bound is a stronger version of convexity; it is often called η\etaη-strong convexity. The second one is indeed nothing more than the fact that if the Hessian norm is bounded by MMM, then the gradient of fff is MMM-Lipschitz. We say that fff is MMM-smooth.

Convergence of the gradient descent dynamic

There are several takeaway messages in this elementary but powerful result.

  • Having a step size ε\varepsilonε smaller than the Lipschitz constant MMM is mandatory to get something meaningful. Otherwise, if the step size is too large, we could take steps that are consistently too big and overshoot the minimum at every step.

  • We don't need fff to be smooth, only that fff is η\etaη-strongly convex and MMM-smooth, or equivalently that (2) holds.

  • Reaching a point at a distance smaller than δ\deltaδ from the minimum xxx will need a number of steps of order ≈κln⁡(1/δ)\approx \kappa \ln(1/\delta)≈κln(1/δ) with κ=M/η\kappa = M/\etaκ=M/η.

  • This quantity κ\kappaκ can be interpreted as a « global » conditionning number of the Hessian of fff: indeed, the conditionning of the Hessian at every point is smaller than κ\kappaκ. The smaller κ\kappaκ, the faster the convergence. Elementary preconditioning methods consist in replacing the objective function fff by x↦f(Px)x\mapsto f(P x)x↦f(Px) for some matrix PPP. The function stays strongly convex and smooth, but the gradient is P∇f(Px)P\nabla f(Px)P∇f(Px) and the Hessian is P∇2f(Px)P∗P\nabla^2 f(Px)P^*P∇2f(Px)P∗; a careful choice of PPP will really reduce the conditionning of the problem.

  • When f(x)=∣Ax−b∣2/2f(x) = |Ax - b|^2 / 2f(x)=∣Ax−b∣2/2, we are doing nothing more than an iterative method to find a solution for Ax=bAx = bAx=b. The gradient of fff is AxAxAx, while the Hessian is AAA: it no longer depends on xxx. Its eigenvalues are the eigenvalues of AAA, hence κ\kappaκ is really the conditioning number of AAA.
