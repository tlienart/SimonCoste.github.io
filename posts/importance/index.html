Importance sampling ⚖️

June 2023

Sampling refers to the generation of random variables following a certain probability distribution; for example if ρ\rhoρ is a probability measure on R\mathbb{R}R, we want to generate random variables which are independent and follow the distribution given by ρ\rhoρ, or we want to compute expectations like E[φ(Y)]\mathbb{E}[\varphi(Y)]E[φ(Y)] for some function φ\varphiφ, where Y∼ρY \sim \rhoY∼ρ. In many cases, one does not fully knows ρ\rhoρ, but only that it is proportional to some function fff, that is ρ(x)=f(x)/Z\rho(x) = f(x)/Zρ(x)=f(x)/Z where Z=∫f(u)duZ = \int f(u)duZ=∫f(u)du, and computing ZZZ (the normalizing constant) is intractable.

There are many techniques that still allow to sample from ρ\rhoρ in this case; in this note I'm focusing on importance sampling (IS), also called reweighting. In this note I prove an insightful result by Chatterjee and Diaconis on the number of samples required for IS to be sufficiently precise.

The basic idea of Importance sampling

Let ν\nuν be probability measure, easy to sample from, with density ggg. Let Y∼ρY \sim \rhoY∼ρ and X∼νX\sim \nuX∼ν (in the sequel, YiY_iYi​ will always have density ρ\rhoρ and XiX_iXi​ density ggg). Then, for any function φ\varphiφ,

E[φ(X)f(X)/g(X)]=∫φ(x)f(x)g(x)g(x)dx=∫φ(x)f(x)dx=ZE[φ(Y)]. \mathbb{E}[\varphi(X)f(X)/g(X)] = \int \varphi(x)\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}g(x)dx = \int \varphi(x)f(x)dx = Z \mathbb{E}[\varphi(Y)]. E[φ(X)f(X)/g(X)]=∫φ(x)g(x)f(x)​g(x)dx=∫φ(x)f(x)dx=ZE[φ(Y)].

Applying this formula to φ≡1\varphi \equiv 1φ≡1 also yields E[f(X)/g(X)]=Z\mathbb{E}[f(X)/g(X)] =ZE[f(X)/g(X)]=Z. Consequently,

E[φ(X)f(X)/g(X)]E[f(X)/g(X)]=E[φ(Y)].\frac{ \mathbb{E}[\varphi(X)f(X)/g(X)] }{ \mathbb{E}[f(X)/g(X)] } = \mathbb{E}[\varphi(Y)].E[f(X)/g(X)]E[φ(X)f(X)/g(X)]​=E[φ(Y)].

This suggests the following method for approximating E[φ(Y)]\mathbb{E}[\varphi(Y)]E[φ(Y)] without computing ZZZ.

Let X1,…,XnX_1, \dotsc, X_nX1​,…,Xn​ be iid with law ν\nuν. Set w(x)=f(x)/g(x)w(x) = f(x)/g(x)w(x)=f(x)/g(x). Then, when n→∞n\to\inftyn→∞, almost surely one has ∑i=1nw(Xi)n→Z \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n w(X_i)}{n} \to Z n∑i=1n​w(Xi​)​→Z and ∑i=1nw(Xi)φ(Xi)∑i=1nw(Xi)→E[φ(Y)]=∫Φ(x)f(x)Zdx. \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n w(X_i) \varphi(X_i)}{\sum_{i=1}^n w(X_i)} \to \mathbb{E}[\varphi(Y)]= \int \Phi(x)\frac{f(x)}{Z}dx.∑i=1n​w(Xi​)∑i=1n​w(Xi​)φ(Xi​)​→E[φ(Y)]=∫Φ(x)Zf(x)​dx.

Proof. By the Law of Large Numbers, the LHS of (3) converges towards E[w(X)]=∫w(x)g(x)dx=∫f(x)dx=Z\mathbf{E}[w(X)] = \int w(x)g(x)dx = \int f(x)dx = ZE[w(X)]=∫w(x)g(x)dx=∫f(x)dx=Z.

Also by the LLN, ∑w(Xi)φ(Xi)n\frac{\sum w(X_i)\varphi(X_i)}{n}n∑w(Xi​)φ(Xi​)​ converges towards E[φ(X)w(X)]=ZE[φ(Y)]\mathbf{E}[\varphi(X)w(X)] = Z\mathbf{E}[\varphi(Y)]E[φ(X)w(X)]=ZE[φ(Y)].

Take the ratio of the two to get (4).

This technique explains the word importance sampling: the samples XiX_iXi​ are iid but their density is g(x)g(x)g(x), not f(x)/Zf(x)/Zf(x)/Z, and the weights correct the difference between the two. If some xxx is very likely under ggg but not so much under fff (meaning that f(x)/Zf(x)/Zf(x)/Z is close to zero but g(x)g(x)g(x) is high), then this sample will be assigned a very small weight.

How good is this approximation?

Unless ρ\rhoρ and ν\nuν are already very close to each other, this can be pretty bad. Let's look at the variance of the estimator Z^\hat{Z}Z^ given in (3): clearly E[Z^]=Z\mathbf{E}[\hat{Z}]=ZE[Z^]=Z, hence

Var(Z^)=∫g(x)f(x)2g(x)2dx−Z2n=∫f(x)f(x)g(x)dx−Z2n=ZE[f(Y)/g(Y)]−Z2n. \mathrm{Var}(\hat{Z}) = \frac{\int g(x) \frac{f(x)^2}{g(x)^2}dx - Z^2}{n} = \frac{\int f(x) \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}dx - Z^2}{n} = \frac{Z\mathbf{E}[f(Y)/g(Y)]-Z^2}{n}.Var(Z^)=n∫g(x)g(x)2f(x)2​dx−Z2​=n∫f(x)g(x)f(x)​dx−Z2​=nZE[f(Y)/g(Y)]−Z2​.

The first term could be prohibitively big. Indeed, let us consider the following situation: our prior density is a Standard Gaussian g(x)=e−x2/2/2πg(x) = e^{-x^2/2}/\sqrt{2\pi}g(x)=e−x2/2/2π​, and our target density is the same, but shifted by 10: f(x)=e−(x−10)2/2/2πf(x) = e^{-(x-10)^2/2}/\sqrt{2\pi}f(x)=e−(x−10)2/2/2π​. Here both densities are normalized, so Z=1Z=1Z=1. But,

E[f(Y)/g(Y)]=12π∫e−(x−10)22e−(x−10)22+x22dx=12π∫e−(x−20)22+100dx=e100.\mathbf{E}[f(Y)/g(Y)] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int e^{-\frac{(x-10)^2}{2}}e^{-\frac{(x-10)^2 }{ 2} + \frac{x^2}{2}}dx = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int e^{- \frac{(x-20)^2}{2} + 100}dx = e^{100}.E[f(Y)/g(Y)]=2π​1​∫e−2(x−10)2​e−2(x−10)2​+2x2​dx=2π​1​∫e−2(x−20)2​+100dx=e100.

That is too big. Having a low variance for Z^\hat{Z}Z^ would require having more than e100e^{100}e100 samples, which is impossible.

The Effective Sample Size

Practically, a good indicator of the quality of our importance sampling is given by the effective sample size. Suppose you used nnn samples XiX_iXi​. If they were distributed exactly as ρ\rhoρ, that is if ggg was proportional to fff, we would have w(Xi)=1/Zw(X_i) = 1/Zw(Xi​)=1/Z for all XiX_iXi​. In general fff is not proportional to ggg hence that's not the case and one way to measure how far the samples we have are from nnn samples of ρ\rhoρ, we set σ^n\hat{\sigma}_nσ^n​ = the empirical coefficient of variation of the w(Xi)w(X_i)w(Xi​), and

ESS(n)=n1+σ^n. \mathrm{ESS}(n) = \frac{n}{1 + \hat{\sigma}_n}.ESS(n)=1+σ^n​n​.

How to use the ESS ?

Suppose that you use IS with a sample size of n=1000n=1000n=1000. If ESS≈1000\mathrm{ESS} \approx 1000ESS≈1000 then the weigths are almost constant and there's a good chance that our sampling is excellent. If it's small, say ESS≈100\mathrm{ESS} \approx 100ESS≈100, then it means that the quality of your IS estimator is the same as if you would have used 100 samples of the real distribution ρ\rhoρ.

This method is a good rule of thumb to assess the quality of IS, but it's quite empirical. In general, what is the required number of samples when one wants to efficiently estimate a mean E[φ(Y)]\mathbf{E}[\varphi(Y)]E[φ(Y)] using importance sampling?

The number of samples required for IS
