Robbins' version of the Stirling approximation

November 2022

The most beautiful formula in mathematics is certainly not the triviality 1+eiπ=01 + e^{i\pi}=01+eiπ=0, as claimed by many tasteless mathematicians with no self-esteem, but rather Stirling's formula, stating that n!∼nne−n2πnn!\sim n^n e^{-n}\sqrt{2\pi n}n!∼nne−n2πn​. Quantifying the error of this approximation can be done to any precision using the Euler-MacLaurin complicated formula, but it is often handy to have a simpler estimation; Herbert Robbins[1] found one (original note here) which is surprisingly accurate:

e112n+1<n!nne−n2πn<e112n. e^{\frac{1}{12n + 1}} < \frac{n!}{n^n e^{-n} \sqrt{2\pi n}} < e^{\frac{1}{12n}}. e12n+11​<nne−n2πn​n!​<e12n1​.

The inequalities are strict and valid for every nnn. The proof of this result is given below. For future reference, we can compare (1) with the higher-order expansion: 

n!nne−n2πn=exp⁡{112n−1360n3+11260n5+O(1n7)}. \frac{n!}{n^n e^{-n}\sqrt{2\pi n}} = \exp \left\lbrace \frac{1}{12n} - \frac{1}{360n^3} + \frac{1}{1260n^5} + O(\frac{1}{n^7}) \right\rbrace.nne−n2πn​n!​=exp{12n1​−360n31​+1260n51​+O(n71​)}.

Proof of (1)

We have ln⁡(n!)=∑k=1n−1ln⁡(k+1)\ln(n!) = \sum_{k=1}^{n-1} \ln(k+1)ln(n!)=∑k=1n−1​ln(k+1). Robbins' proof consists in a subtle approximation of ln⁡(k+1)\ln(k+1)ln(k+1), as seen as the area of the rectangle [k,k+1]×[0,ln⁡(k+1)][k,k+1] \times [0, \ln(k+1)][k,k+1]×[0,ln(k+1)]. This area is equal to: 

  • the area IkI_kIk​ of the points in the rectangle under the curve ln⁡(x)\ln(x)ln(x), that is ∫kk+1ln⁡(x)dx\int_k^{k+1}\ln(x)dx∫kk+1​ln(x)dx, (in the picture, it's the pink+black zone)

  • plus the area TkT_kTk​ of the triangle between the points (k,ln⁡(k)),(k,ln⁡(k+1)),(k+1,ln⁡(k+1))(k,\ln(k)), (k, \ln(k+1)), (k+1, \ln(k+1))(k,ln(k)),(k,ln(k+1)),(k+1,ln(k+1)), (blue+black zone)

  • minus the area δk\delta_kδk​ of the small black zone we counted twice, which is equal to IkI_kIk​ minus the area of the points in the rectangle that are not in the preceding triangle.

Clearly, Tk=(ln⁡(k+1)−ln⁡(k))/2T_k = (\ln(k+1) - \ln(k))/2Tk​=(ln(k+1)−ln(k))/2. We also recall that the antiderative of ln⁡(x)\ln(x)ln(x) is xln⁡(x)−xx\ln(x) - xxln(x)−x. Consequently, noting Sn=δ2+⋯+δn−1S_n = \delta_2 + \dotsb + \delta_{n-1}Sn​=δ2​+⋯+δn−1​,
