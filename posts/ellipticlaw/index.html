An inverse visualization for the elliptic law

March 2021

Here are two nice facts on spectra of random matrices of large size nnn.

  • When AAA is a Girko matrix, the eigenvalues of A/nA/\sqrt{n}A/n​ asymptotically follow the circular law – they tend to be uniformly distributed in D=D(0,1)\mathbb{D} = D(0,1)D=D(0,1). By Girko matrix, I mean a matrix where all the entries are iid real[1] random variables, centered, with variance 1.

  • When AAA is a Wigner matrix, the eigenvalues of A/nA/\sqrt{n}A/n​ asymptotically follow a semi-circle law, and in particular they are real. By Wigner matrix, I mean a symmetric matrix where all the entries on and above the diagonal are iid real random variables, centered, with variance 1.

What happens in between lies in the elliptic realm. If AAA is a Wigner matrix, the symmetry of AAA is indeed equivalent to AAA being maximally correlated with its own transpose A∗A^*A∗, since they are equal. One can thus create intermediary models between Girko and Wigner, by parametrizing the degree of correlation above and below the diagonal: for this, we just take AAA to be a fully independent matrix (Girko), and set

Xρ=A+ρA∗.X_\rho = A + \rho A^*.Xρ​=A+ρA∗.

The matrix X0=AX_0 = AX0​=A is a Girko matrix; the matrix X1=A+A∗X_1 = A + A^*X1​=A+A∗ is a symmetric matrix and the matrix X−1=A−A∗X_{-1} = A - A^*X−1​=A−A∗ it is an antisymmetric matrix. In XρX_\rhoXρ​, the entries above and below the diagonal are correlated, in that if i≠ji \neq ji=j,
